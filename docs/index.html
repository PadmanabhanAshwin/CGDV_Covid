India

India, as of September 2020, is the fastest growing outbreak in the world with over 75,000 new cases reported each day. With already shrinking growth going into 2020, COVID-19 has had a debilitating effect on the Indian economy with GDP plummeting 24%, the steepest fall in it's history. Recent figures released by the Indian Government show transport and hotel sectors have shrunk 50% since last year. India's once mighty manufacturing industry has fallen 40%. The COVID crisis has also exposed the deep economic divide in the country. Migrant workers, who make up more that 80% of the national workforce and responsible for building the country's ever-expanding infrastructure, have been one of the most severely affected communities; unable to pay rents in expensive cities without work, more than half a million migrant workers left for their rural hometowns, often more than thousand miles away, on foot. Several hundred people have been reported to have died due to exhaustion, starvation and dehydration during their journey. With looming recession, dwindling consumer demand and exponentially increasing COVID 19 cases, all in the backdrop of communal tensions and border disputes, India faces a tough challenge in the coming months.

Brazil

Brazil, with the second-highest total number of cases globally is among the countries worst affected by COVID worldwide. Brazil's economic growth has fallen two consecutive quarters, with the Central Bank of Brazil expecting an overall 5.3% contraction in 2020. With the Bolsa Família (family allowance) program, the Brazilian government has provided aid in the form of monthly payments, of about $100 to low-income families, a very popular move for the ruling government. Though these payments have helped millions of families, economist predict the Brazilian debt will rise to an astonishing 95% of the nations' GDP by the end of 2020. The Brazilian finance ministry and industries have warned against generous government spending to keep the economy stable and attractive to investors. Despite the death rate dropping slightly from 1000 in June to 900 late August, the pandemic in Brazil is far from over. Social caution and media coverage for COVID has seen a sharp decline over the last month, with large crowds gatherings in beaches and pubs.

Pakistan

Pakistan over the past month seems to have taken the right steps to curtail the pandemic. The country recorded just 230 new cases as of August 31st, it's lowest in the four months. While reports that suggest that the not enough number of tests, a significant reduction in the positivity rate suggests things are indeed improving. The Karachi Stock Market 100 recorded a dollar-adjust return of 38%, making it the best performing market in Asia, over China and India, and the fourth best in the world. The strong performance is attributed to Pakistan Central Bank's aggressive stance in cutting interest rates and improving cash-flow in the economy. The pandemic does seem to be dealing a blow to Pakistan's fledgling democracy. Unhappy with the initial COVID response by the government, the powerful military leadership in Pakistan has taken center stage in responding to the outbreak. This shift in power threatens to destabilize the incumbent government. This reorganization of power has also led to crackdown on dissent and freedom of speech; prominent journalists expressing concern about the pandemic have been known to be persecuted and in some cases abducted.
